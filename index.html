Course Description

What makes for a responsibly designed product? What duties do technologists have to ensure system security and protect user privacy? Who should be held accountable when artificial intelligence misbehaves? What constraints does intellectual property law place on product design and technologists’ latitude to experiment with technologies? As new technologies continue to emerge and shape both our lives and the world around us, they also raise novel, complex ethical questions that today’s engineers, tech entrepreneurs, other technology professionals are forced to confront.

This course will introduce the legal frameworks and principles of ethical decision-making that enable technologists to identify their societal responsibilities, and to think critically through difficult ethical issues. Each class, we will exercise the application of these principles as we examine and reason through real world ethical dilemmas at the cutting edge of technology, approaching the questions they raise from the perspectives of the technology professionals who actually face them. Topics that will be explored this semester include cybersecurity, data protection, digital surveillance, artificial intelligence, environmental sustainability, open-source software, autonomous vehicles, and more.

This section satisfies the Engineering Ethics Requirement for CIS students.

Learning Outcomes

At the end of this course, students should be able to:

  • Identify and critically analyze the ethical and/or legal issues implicated by an emerging technology or within the technology sector.
  • Demonstrate a core understanding of the interplay between law and ethics in decision-making affecting technologists.
  • Evaluate the potential consequences of introducing a new technology, the impact of design choices, and the tradeoffs involved.
  • Develop and effectively communicate well-reasoned, normative arguments in response to a technology ethics or policy issue.

Course Materials

There is no required textbook for this course. Save the money and buy yourself something nice. All reading materials for this course will be made available on the course website. I have provided the appropriate URL address in the syllabus below, but if for some reason you are unable to access a particular reading, please let me know.

Note on the Readings

Pay attention to the page ranges or section numbers designated for each assignment, as there are many instances where I only intend for you to read part of the material listed. These will be found in brackets at the end of a bulleted item in the reading schedule below. Also note that many readings have been designated as optional. You are not expected to complete these readings, nor should you feel pressured to do so (it will have no effect on your grade). These relate to material that I will likely mention in our class discussions and are only listed so you can go back reference them afterwards if so curious.

Grading Breakdown

Three short writing assignments (20% each, or 60% total) - These assignments will require you to write a short (3-6 pages, double-spaced) essay or memo based on a topic/writing prompt that I have provided. The assignments will be due as indicated in the Syllabus (see below for exact dates). For each assignment, I will provide a full explanation of the topic two weeks before it is due.

Final exam (25%) - You will be given a take-home, open-book exam that must be submitted within the designated time window during finals week. I will provide further details about the exam structure and content later in the semester.

Participation (15%) - This course will incorporate in-class discussions as a core component. A non-negligible portion of your grade will depend on your active participation in these discussions. Therefore, you are strongly encouraged to come to class prepared by reading all materials for that week beforehand.
