Course Description

What makes for a responsibly designed product? What duties do technologists’ have to ensure system security and protect user privacy? Who should be held accountable when artificial intelligence misbehaves? What constraints does intellectual property law place on product design and technologists’ latitude to experiment with technologies? As new technologies continue to emerge and shape both our lives and the world around us, they also raise novel, complex ethical questions that today’s engineers, tech entrepreneurs, other technology professionals are forced to confront.

This course will introduce the legal frameworks and principles of ethical decision-making that enable technologists to identify their societal responsibilities and think critically through difficult ethical issues. Each class, we will exercise the application of these principles by examining real, modern ethical dilemmas at the cutting edge of technology, approaching them from the perspective of a practicing technologist. Major topics we will explore this semester include cybersecurity and privacy, digital surveillance, artificial intelligence, environmental sustainability, open source software, autonomous vehicles, and more.

Learning Outcomes

Learning outcomes for the semester include the following:

  • Demonstrate a core understanding of major legal and ethical considerations in the technology sector.
  • Be able to spot key legal and ethical issues pertaining to technology as utilized in the professional environment.
  • Communicate effectively on topics related to technology law and ethics.
  • Acquire an understanding of the interplay between law and ethics in decision-making affecting technologists.

Course Materials

There is no required textbook for this course. Save the money and buy yourself something nice. All reading materials for this course will be made available on the course website. For internet readings, I have provided the appropriate URL address in the syllabus below.

Note on the Readings

Many of the assigned readings include excerpts from articles and cases. Note the page ranges designated for each assignment. In some cases, I have specified section numbers where page ranges can be confusing to those not yet familiar with case pagination standards.

Grade Breakdown

Grades will be based on three writing assignments (15% each, or 45% total), one in-class oral presentation (15%), class participation (15%), and a final exam (25%).

Short writing assignments - assignments in the form of 4-6 page essays will be due as indicated in the Syllabus (see below for exact dates and assigned topics). I will provide a full explanation of each topic two weeks before it is due.

Presentations - Presentation opportunities will be spread throughout the semester. Students may request their desired topic, and I will do my best to give everyone their first or second choice. The presentation shall include a clear presentation of the relevant technological, ethical, and/or legal issues, an analysis of these issues utilizing class knowledge, and a proposed resolution.

Participation - This course will incorporate in-class discussions as a core component. A non-negligible portion of your grade will depend on your active participation in these discussions. Therefore, you are strongly encouraged to come to class prepared by reading all materials for that week beforehand.
